# Composition Systems

Composition is a decision, not a default. Pick a system first; let elements follow.

## How To Use

1. Pick **one** composition system based on task type (see table below).
2. Pick **one** reading path (how the eye should travel) and make sure the system supports it.
3. Only break the system for a deliberate reason; document the reason.

## The Seven Systems

### 1. Rule of Thirds

- **What**: divide into 3×3; place key elements on the four intersections.
- **When to use**: editorial, lifestyle, landscape, most portrait work.
- **Why it works**: avoids the visual boredom of dead-center while staying predictable.
- **Prompt cues**: "rule of thirds", "subject on right third line", "horizon on lower third".
- **When to break**: when you explicitly need frontal authority (ID portrait, luxury product hero).

### 2. Golden Section / Phi

- **What**: 1.618 proportion; φ-grid with a spiral reading path.
- **When to use**: editorial spreads, classical fine-art-leaning work, book covers.
- **Why it works**: spiral supports natural eye travel; feels crafted.
- **Prompt cues**: "golden ratio composition", "spiral leading into face", "fibonacci crop".
- **When to break**: dense info layouts where a grid is more honest.

### 3. Central Symmetry

- **What**: subject in the geometric center; mirror balance on both sides.
- **When to use**: ruler/authority brands, portraits of power, architecture, luxury, tech launch shots.
- **Why it works**: communicates stability, confidence, premium intention.
- **Prompt cues**: "centered symmetrical composition", "perfectly frontal", "mirrored balance".
- **When to break**: when subject needs to feel approachable or in motion.

### 4. Diagonal / Dutch Tilt

- **What**: dominant diagonal line runs corner-to-corner; frame can also be tilted.
- **When to use**: energy, urgency, sports, chaos, tension.
- **Why it works**: diagonals carry more visual energy than horizontals or verticals.
- **Prompt cues**: "strong diagonal composition", "dutch angle", "leading line from lower-left to upper-right".
- **When to break**: when the message is calm or authoritative.

### 5. Frame Within Frame

- **What**: foreground element (doorway, window, hand, arch) frames the real subject.
- **When to use**: storytelling, cinematic, layered information.
- **Why it works**: adds depth, directs attention, creates intimacy.
- **Prompt cues**: "frame within frame", "view through doorway", "foreground silhouette framing subject".
- **When to break**: when clarity of a single isolated subject matters more (product packshot).

### 6. Negative Space / Minimalist

- **What**: subject occupies < 30% of the frame; the rest is intentional void.
- **When to use**: luxury, calm, wellness, premium minimalism, editorial covers.
- **Why it works**: the void amplifies the subject and signals confidence.
- **Prompt cues**: "80% negative space", "single subject in lower right corner", "vast empty sky".
- **When to break**: when you need to convey density (maximalist playful, information-heavy).

### 7. Maximalist / Fill-the-Frame

- **What**: every inch is loaded; layered collage, pattern-on-pattern.
- **When to use**: Gen Z Y2K revival, festival poster, sale event, rebel brands.
- **Why it works**: creates visceral density; feels alive.
- **Prompt cues**: "maximalist collage", "layered stickers and cutouts", "edge-to-edge pattern".
- **When to break**: when any of trust / calm / luxury is a goal.

## Reading Paths (Visual Flow)

Pick a path and design the composition to enforce it.

### Z-Pattern

- Eye scans top-left → top-right → diagonal → bottom-left → bottom-right.
- **Use for**: simple pages with a clear CTA, landing pages, posters with one headline.
- **Anchor placement**: logo top-left, support top-right, CTA bottom-right.

### F-Pattern

- Eye scans horizontally across the top, then halfway across further down, then down the left edge.
- **Use for**: dense text, article layout, menus, info-heavy posters.
- **Anchor placement**: strongest message in the top bar; important items on left edge.

### Gutenberg Diagram

- Eye starts primary optical area (top-left), sweeps down to terminal area (bottom-right), with weak / fallow areas in the other two corners.
- **Use for**: long-form editorial pages in left-to-right scripts.
- **Anchor placement**: start with an anchor image top-left, end with CTA bottom-right.

### Circular / Loop

- Eye circles around a focal point.
- **Use for**: symmetric luxury composition, hero product on plinth, radial layouts.
- **Anchor placement**: subject at the circle's center; orbital elements guide return.

### Convergent

- Multiple leading lines converge on a single focal point.
- **Use for**: portrait in environment, architectural hero, dramatic moments.
- **Anchor placement**: convergence point = the one thing that must be noticed.

## Aspect Ratios (what to use where)

| Ratio | Typical use |
|---|---|
| 1:1 | Instagram feed, Xiaohongshu square, LinkedIn post |
| 4:5 | Instagram portrait, mobile-first feed |
| 3:4 | Xiaohongshu portrait (primary), Pinterest |
| 9:16 | Story, Reels, TikTok, Douyin, phone lock-screen wallpaper |
| 16:9 | YouTube, desktop web hero, presentation slide |
| 2:3 | Print poster, magazine cover, Pinterest pin |
| 2.35:1 / 2.39:1 | cinematic banner, website full-bleed hero |
| 3:2 | DSLR native, reportage photography |

Do not let aspect ratio be an afterthought. Choosing the wrong ratio is the single most common reason "beautiful" images fail on-platform.

## Visual Weight & Balance

Elements carry weight. Balance means weights on opposite sides roughly equal.

Weight drivers, from strongest to weakest:

1. High color contrast with the field
2. High saturation
3. Large size
4. Human face (always heavy)
5. Sharp edge vs. blurred edge
6. Warm color on cool field
7. Text (always heavier than its visual size)

Rule: text weighs more than it looks. A small block of text will consistently outweigh a much larger neutral image area.

## Hierarchy (the 3-level test)

Every composition should pass this test:

- **Level 1 (0.5s glance)**: one thing dominates. What is it?
- **Level 2 (3s)**: what does the viewer read/see next? Can you point at it?
- **Level 3 (10s+)**: is there a detail that rewards lingering?

If all three collapse into one flat field, the hierarchy has failed — regenerate, don't accept.

## When To Break the System

Breaking the rules is fine. Breaking them accidentally is not.

Legitimate reasons to break:

1. You want tension as the message.
2. The brand's archetype demands a formal break (Outlaw, Jester).
3. A cultural convention requires a different system (e.g., traditional East-Asian asymmetric balance vs. Western central symmetry).
4. Platform constraint overrules the aesthetic optimum.

Document the break in your brief so future iterations do not accidentally "fix" it.
